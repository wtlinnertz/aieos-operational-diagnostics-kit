# TERMS

This document defines common terms used throughout the **aieos-reliability-resilience-kit** repository. These definitions are intentionally **tool-agnostic**, **employer-neutral**, and **AI-friendly**.

---

## Core Concepts

### Service Level Objective (SLO)
A measurable reliability target for a service, expressed as a percentage over a defined measurement window. An SLO defines what "working correctly" means in a way that is objective and testable. Example: "99.9% of requests succeed over a 30-day rolling window."

---

### Service Level Indicator (SLI)
The specific metric being measured to determine SLO compliance. An SLI is a quantitative measure of service behavior — not an opinion. Example: the ratio of successful HTTP responses to total HTTP responses.

---

### Error Budget
The allowable unreliability defined by an SLO. Calculated as 100% minus the SLO target. A 99.9% SLO has a 0.1% error budget — meaning 43.2 minutes of downtime per 30-day window is acceptable. The error budget is not a goal; it is a limit.

---

### Burn Rate
The rate at which the error budget is being consumed relative to the measurement window pace. A burn rate of 1× means the budget is being consumed at exactly the sustainable pace. A burn rate of 14× means the budget will be exhausted in 1/14th of the measurement window if the rate continues.

---

### Measurement Window
The time period over which SLO compliance is calculated. Common windows: 30-day rolling, 28-day rolling, weekly. The measurement window must be explicit — "99.9% availability" is incomplete; "99.9% availability over a 30-day rolling window" is complete.

---

### Freeze Condition
The SRP-defined error budget consumption level at which new feature releases affecting the service are frozen. Defined per SLO in the SRP. When crossed, releases require explicit exception authorization.

---

## Artifact Types

### SRER — Service Reliability Entry Record
The entry gate for the Reliability & Resilience Kit. Human-authored. Confirms the upstream Release Record is frozen, names the reliability owner, confirms monitoring is active, and captures the SLO baseline from the RR §7 handoff.

The SRER is not generated by AI.

---

### SRP — Service Reliability Profile
The foundational reliability artifact defining what "working correctly" means for a service: SLO targets, error budgets, burn rate alert thresholds, and measurement methodology.

The SRP is versioned and reused until SLOs change. When SLOs change, a new SRP version is issued; the previous version remains frozen. All IRs and RHRs reference the SRP version active at their time.

---

### IR — Incident Record
A per-incident evidence artifact documenting what happened during an incident from detection through resolution and post-incident review. The IR is not a post-mortem template — it is an evidence artifact with specific hard gates (classification, impact, timeline, root cause, remediation, SLO impact).

---

### RHR — Reliability Health Report
A periodic review artifact synthesizing SLO compliance data, error budget state, and incident history over a coverage period. The terminal artifact of a review period; produces the Layer 7 feed.

---

### RR — Release Record
The terminal artifact of the Release & Exposure Kit (Layer 5). The RR §7 Handoff to Layer 6 section is the upstream boundary contract for this kit.

---

## Incident Concepts

### Severity
The impact classification of an incident, selected from an enumerated list: SEV1 (complete outage) | SEV2 (major degradation) | SEV3 (partial degradation) | SEV4 (minor issue). Severity determines response urgency and resource allocation.

---

### Detection Method
How an incident was first identified: automated alert | user report | engineer observation | external notification. Detection method is required in the IR because it informs whether alerting coverage is adequate.

---

### Proximate Cause
The immediate trigger of an incident — what happened that caused the incident to begin. Distinct from root cause.

---

### Root Cause
The underlying condition that made the proximate cause possible. An effective root cause analysis identifies the proximate cause AND the contributing systemic factors that enabled it. "Human error" alone is not a root cause.

---

### Mitigation
The action taken to reduce or stop the impact of an incident, which may not address the underlying root cause. A service restart may be mitigation. Fixing the bug that required the restart is resolution.

---

### Resolution
The action taken to address the root cause and restore normal operation. Resolution is distinct from mitigation and must be identifiable in the incident timeline.

---

### MTTR — Mean Time to Resolution
The average time from incident detection to resolution across a set of incidents. A reliability improvement metric tracked across RHR periods.

---

## Reliability Concepts

### SLO Compliance
The fraction of time (or requests, or events) during a measurement period that met the SLO target. Expressed as a percentage. Compliance is a measurement, not an assertion.

---

### Error Budget Consumption
The portion of the error budget used during a measurement period. Expressed as a percentage of the total budget and as a time equivalent. Example: "2.4% of the 30-day delivery rate error budget = 10.3 minutes."

---

### Layer 7 Feed
The §5 section of the Reliability Health Report that provides reliability trend data, systemic issue assessment, improvement signals, and recommended watch items for downstream consumption by the Insight & Evolution Kit (Layer 7).

---

## Validation

### Hard Gate
A specific, binary pass/fail criterion in a spec. All hard gates must pass for a validator to return PASS status. A single failing gate fails the artifact.

---

### Standard Enforceability
A property of the SRP: all standards must be specific enough to evaluate compliance without judgment. Standards using "should," "ideally," "when possible," or equivalent aspirational language fail this criterion.

---

## Key Principles

- **AI generates, validators decide**
- **SLOs are measurable commitments, not aspirations**
- **Freeze before promote — upstream artifacts must be frozen before downstream generation**
- **SRP is versioned — changes require a new version, not editing a frozen artifact**
- **Evidence is concrete, timestamped, traceable, and retrievable**
- **Separate generation and validation sessions to prevent self-validation bias**

---

## Notes

- These terms are intentionally generic and tool-agnostic
- No terminology implies a specific employer, vendor, or monitoring platform
- Examples in this repository use placeholder names and neutral contexts
